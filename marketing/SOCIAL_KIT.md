## Read before you post (positioning guardrails)

- **Builder PM framing.** "I designed and built it end to end." True: solo, with AI-assisted coding on a serverless stack. Don't dress it up beyond that.
- **No inflated claims.** It just went live with a public demo. Do not claim users, downloads, revenue, or traction you don't have. "I built this, here's why, try it" is the honest and stronger move.
- **If you cite experience,** say "6+ years shipping platform products in cross-functional roles," never a bare "6+ years PM."
- **Voice:** no em dashes, no emoji, plain language. It reads more credible and keeps the AI-tell away.
- **Link:** `keel-aba.pages.dev` (swap for a custom domain if you wire one). The "Try the live demo" button is on that page.

## LinkedIn (primary, dual-purpose)

I consider myself a disciplined person, and I still quit every expense tracker I tried.

Not because the math was wrong. I'd miss a few entries, the totals would drift, and the app started to feel like a lie. So I'd stop. When I asked around, almost everyone had quit theirs the same way.

That pointed at the real problem: it isn't willpower, it's design. Every tracker punishes a missed entry instead of absorbing it.

So I built Keel.

The premise: a missed entry never breaks your totals. It quietly becomes "uncategorized," and you settle it later at a calm checkpoint I named Harbour. You see what's safe to spend, you can fall behind without guilt, and you catch up when you want. It's private by default (no SMS or bank scraping), and voice entry is tuned for India, so logging a spend is one sentence.

I designed and built it end to end. It's live now, with a public demo and no sign-up:

keel-aba.pages.dev

If you've ever abandoned a budgeting app, I'd genuinely like to know what made you quit. That's the problem I'm trying to kill.
